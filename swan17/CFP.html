3rd International Workshop on Software Analytics (SWAN 2017)

 
 

Many prominent tech companies have embraced an analytics-driven culture to help improve their decision making. Analytics include methods of gathering, preprocessing, transforming and modelling raw data with the purpose of highlighting useful information and drawing conclusions from it. Software analytics are used to leverage large volumes of data from multiple sources to help practitioners make informed decisions about their projects. While analytics solutions demonstrated promising results, there are many challenges left concerned with developing, integrating, adopting analytics into software development processes.

The third International Workshop on Software Analytics (SWAN 2017) aims at providing a common venue for researchers and practitioners across software engineering, data mining and mining software repositories research domains to share new approaches and emerging results in developing and validating analytics rich solutions, as well as adopting analytics to software development and maintenance processes to better inform their everyday decisions. The goals of the workshop are to discuss progress on software analytics, data mining and analysis; to gather empirical evidence on the use and effectiveness of analytics; and to identify priorities for a research agenda. The workshop invites both academic researchers and industrial practitioners for an exchange of ideas and collaboration.

             


This year we are announcing a specialized theme “unsupervised learning” for submissions. However, we also accept papers on general topics of software analytics. Topics of interest include, but are not limited to:

  1. •Applications of software and data analytics to support decision making;

  2. •Data-driven approaches for data exploration and analysis;

  3. •Predictive analytics;

  4. •Analytics in model-based development;

  5. •Web analytics, development analytics, business intelligence tools, Hadoop tools;

  6. •Quantitative vs. qualitative analytics;

  7. •Large-scale data mining, analysis and analytics;

  8. •Software analytics for various stakeholders (e.g., managers vs. developers);

  9. •Methods of integrating data from multiple sources (applications, interfaces, mobile apps);

  10. •Empirical studies on how software analytics are used in practice and their effectiveness;

  11. •Negative results ("what did not work") when adopting software analytics, and experience reports;

  12. •Identification of open research challenges and proposed solutions.





SWAN 2017 invites contributions in the form of short (4-page) and full (7-page) papers both research and industry. All submissions should describe unpublished work and must have been neither previously accepted for publication nor concurrently submitted for review in another journal, book, conference, or workshop. Submissions can be position papers, research papers, studies, experience or practice reports.

Submission site: http://tiny.cc/swan17

All submissions must conform to the ACM proceedings formatting guidelines, see and must not exceed 7 pages (including all text, figures). If the submission is accepted, at least one author must attend the workshop and present the paper in order for the paper to be published in the ACM DL proceedings. Paper submissions should be in PDF format, uploaded somewhere online with its URL being submitted to http://tiny.cc/swan17 by May 19, 2017 (AoE Time). Please read below important notes on the double-blind and open review process.

SWAN-17 will use a 1) double-blind, 2) open review process to process submissions.

1) Double-blind review

To facilitate this, authors need to ensure that their manuscripts are prepared in a way that does not give away their identity:

  1. •Authors should remove names and affiliations under the title within the manuscript.

  2. •Use the third person to refer to work the authors have previously undertaken, e.g., replace any phrases like “as we have shown before” with “as it has been shown before [Anonymous, 2007]”.

  3. •Ensure figures do not contain any affiliation related identifiers.

  4. •Do not eliminate essential self-references or other references but limit self-references only to papers that are relevant for those reviewing the submitted paper.

  5. •Cite papers published by the Author in the text as follows: “[Anonymous, 2007]”.

  6. •For blinding in the reference list: “[Anonymous 2007] Details omitted for double-blind reviewing.”

  7. •Remove references to funding sources.

  8. •Do not include acknowledgements in the original sub-
    mission.

  9. •Remove any identifying information, including author names, from file names and ensure document proper- ties are also anonymized.

  10. •Do not link to any repository or on-line archive.

Note that authors, if they wish, may also post their submission, with names, links, full citations, acknowledgements, etc., to public domain paper servers such as arxiv.org. Our reviewers will be specifically instructed to not to search that cite for the papers they are reviewing. That said, authors wishing to increase the probability that their double-blind identify will not be revealed, might consider not posting to sites such as arxiv.org.

  1. 2)Open review

We will use public GitHub repositories to organize our review process.

  1. •Authors and reviewers will be required to create new GitHub IDs that do not reveal any personnel details.

  2. •Those IDs will be added as“writers”to a public GitHub repo.

  3. •Authors will submit their double-blinded papers by (a) placing their papers on-line; then (b) entering the URL of those papers into http://tiny.cc/swan17.

  4. •All reviewing will occur, in public, in public GitHub issue reports.

  5. •There will be no “rebuttal period” since authors will be free to reply to reviewer comments at any time. For rebuttals, authors may include text snippets on how they would modify parts of their paper in order to address reviewer comments. PC co-chairs will monitor the reply process and, if the volume grows too large, they will step in (translation: no flaming in public).

  6. •The current status of a paper (e.g., awaiting 2 reviews, awaiting 1 review, accepted, unaccepted) will be publicly displayed.

  7. •The PC co-chairs reserve the right to run private back channels from reviewers to discuss matters of extreme delicacy. The PC co-chairs undertake to use this channel very rarely and, for most papers, not at all.

  8. •Upon completion of reviews, all review discussions will remain on-line.
